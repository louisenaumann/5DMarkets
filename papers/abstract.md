5DMarkets: A Distributed Computational Theory of Capital Flow Dynamics in Financial Systems

# Abstract

Financial markets are traditionally modeled as stochastic systems in which future price movements are predicted through statistical inference over historical observations. Existing quantitative frameworks generally assume price dynamics as the fundamental object of analysis and treat market behavior as partially random, noisy, and inherently unpredictable.

This paper proposes an alternative theoretical framework.

We introduce 5DMarkets, a physics-constrained computational framework which models financial markets as distributed computational systems governed by deterministic capital flow dynamics. Rather than treating price as the primary variable, we propose that capital allocation represents the true underlying conserved quantity of financial systems, while price emerges as a delayed observable consequence of hidden capital redistribution.

Inspired by five-dimensional computed tomography motion modeling in radiation oncology, the framework models markets as continuously evolving dynamical systems whose future behavior depends on latent state reconstruction, nonlinear manifold geometry, topological phase transitions, hysteresis memory effects, cross-asset deformation propagation, and distributed computational decision-making across heterogeneous market participants.

We propose that financial markets continuously compute future capital allocation through decentralized information processing performed by institutions, algorithmic trading systems, and individual market participants. Capital movement emerges as the physical manifestation of this distributed computation, while price formation emerges secondarily from capital concentration dynamics.

The resulting framework replaces traditional stochastic price prediction with a deterministic state evolution model whose objective is estimation of future capital migration trajectories and optimal interception of capital redistribution before price fully reflects underlying market state changes.

This work proposes a novel interdisciplinary theory connecting dynamical systems physics, computational finance, information theory, complex systems, and distributed computation to describe financial market behavior from first principles.
