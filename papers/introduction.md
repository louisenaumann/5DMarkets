# Introduction

Modern quantitative finance is built upon a set of assumptions which have remained largely unchanged for decades.

Most existing financial models assume that asset prices represent the primary object of analysis. Future market behavior is typically modeled through stochastic processes in which price movements are treated as partially random, noisy, and only probabilistically predictable.

Classical approaches such as random walk theory, Brownian motion models, efficient market hypothesis formulations, and mean variance optimization all fundamentally assume that price dynamics constitute the central mathematical object governing financial systems.

This paper argues that these assumptions are fundamentally incomplete.

We propose that financial markets should not be modeled as stochastic price systems.

Instead, financial markets should be modeled as distributed computational systems in which heterogeneous agents continuously process information, collectively compute future capital allocation decisions, and dynamically redistribute capital throughout an interconnected financial network.

Under this view, price does not represent the primary system variable.

Price emerges secondarily as an observable consequence of hidden capital redistribution occurring throughout the market.

The central hypothesis of this work is that financial markets behave analogously to physical dynamical systems whose evolution can be modeled through continuous state transition equations, memory dependent hysteresis effects, nonlinear manifold geometry, topological phase transitions, and time dependent deformation propagation between economically coupled assets.

Inspired by five dimensional computed tomography motion modeling originally developed for respiratory motion reconstruction in radiation oncology, we introduce a new mathematical framework for modeling market state evolution through deterministic capital flow dynamics.

The resulting framework, called 5DMarkets, attempts to unify concepts from physics, computational finance, distributed computation, complex systems theory, and information processing into a single mathematical description of financial market evolution.
